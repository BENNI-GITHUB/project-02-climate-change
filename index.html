Climate & Causes of Climate Change:

All of the gases on the planet make up the Earth's atmosphere. The pattern of weather conditions over many years is called climate. Climate change refers to long-term alterations in Earth's average weather patterns, including temperature, precipitation, and wind patterns. These changes are primarily driven by human activities, particularly the emission of greenhouse gases such as Carbon Dioxide (CO2) and Methane (CH4) into the atmosphere.

Causes of Climate Change:

  • Greenhouse Gas Emissions: The burning of fossil fuels (like coal, oil, and natural gas) for energy, transportation, and industrial processes releases CO2 and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.These gases trap heat, leading to a gradual increase in global temperatures, known as global warming.
  • Deforestation: Cutting down forests reduces the planet's ability to absorb CO2, contributing to increased greenhouse gas concentrations.
  • Agriculture: Agricultural practices, such as livestock farming and the use of synthetic fertilizers, release methane and nitrous oxide, potent greenhouse gases.

Climate & Causes of Climate Change:

Children's health is a critical concern in the context of climate change, and proactive efforts are essential to protect their well-being in a changing climate.Climate change poses significant risks to children's health, both directly and indirectly. As the Earth's climate continues to change, it exacerbates existing health challenges and introduces new threats that disproportionately affect the most vulnerable, including children. Here are some key ways in which climate change impacts children's health:

  • Lack of Access to Healthcare Due to Migration: Climate change-induced events such as extreme weather, sea-level rise, and droughts can force families and communities to migrate. In many cases, this migration can lead to the displacement of children and their families, resulting in a loss of access to healthcare services.
  • Shortened Food Supply and Poor Nutrition: Climate change disrupts agricultural patterns and reduces crop yields due to factors like increased temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and extreme weather events.
  • Limited Access to Clean Water and Increased Waterborne Illness: Altered rainfall patterns and prolonged droughts can affect the availability and quality of water sources, making it difficult for communities, particularly children, to access clean and safe drinking water.

The Paris Agreement:

The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, is a landmark international treaty aimed at addressing climate change and its impacts.it marked a historic moment in global climate action by bringing together nearly all countries in a collective effort to combat climate change. Its flexible framework allows countries to set their own targets and strategies while promoting global cooperation and accountability. The agreement is seen as a critical tool in the global fight against climate change and has been ratified by the majority of the world's nations. Countries that are parties to the agreement agreed to several key commitments and goals, including:

  • Limiting Global Warming: The central goal of the Paris Agreement is to limit the increase in global average temperature to well below 2 degrees Celsius (3.6 degrees Fahrenheit) above pre-industrial levels, with an aspirational target of limiting the increase to 1.5 degrees Celsius. This is to avoid the most severe and catastrophic impacts of climate change.
  • Emissions Reduction: Each country that is a party to the agreement commits to reducing its greenhouse gas emissions. Parties determine their own targets, called "nationally determined contributions" (NDCs), which outline their specific emission reduction goals. These targets are intended to be ambitious and progressively updated to reflect increased efforts over time.
  • Financial Support: Developed countries committed to providing financial assistance to developing countries to help them mitigate and adapt to the impacts of climate change. This support is crucial for helping vulnerable nations build resilience and transition to low-carbon economies.

What can we do?

Effective steps to stop climate change

Individuals can play a crucial role in addressing climate change by taking a variety of actions to reduce their carbon footprint and advocate for broader change. Here are some effective steps people can take to contribute to the effort to stop climate change:

Reduce Energy Consumption:

  • Use energy-efficient appliances and lighting.
  • Properly insulate homes and buildings to reduce heating and cooling needs.
  • Turn off lights and electronics when not in use.

Conserve Water:

  • Fix leaky faucets and pipes.
  • Install water-efficient fixtures.
  • Use water-saving practices like taking shorter showers and using a broom instead of a hose to clean driveways.

Reduce, Reuse, Recycle:

  • Minimize waste by recycling paper, cardboard, glass, and plastic.
  • Reuse items when possible instead of buying new.
  • Reduce single-use plastics and opt for reusable alternatives.

Sustainable Transportation:

  • Use public transportation, carpool, or bike whenever possible.
  • Choose fuel-efficient vehicles or consider electric vehicles (EVs).
  • Reduce unnecessary air travel and choose video conferencing when feasible.

Support Renewable Energy:

  • Advocate for clean energy policies and incentives.
  • Consider purchasing renewable energy through green energy programs offered by utility companies.

Reduce Meat Consumption:

  • Reduce meat consumption, particularly beef and lamb, as livestock farming is a major source of greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Choose plant-based or lower-impact protein sources.
